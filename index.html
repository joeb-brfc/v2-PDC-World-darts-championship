Founding of the PDC

The Professional Darts Corporation (PDC) was founded in 1992 by a group of leading players who broke away from the British Darts Organisation (BDO). The sport had been struggling during the late 1980s and early 1990s due to declining TV coverage, sponsorship, and media interest. Sixteen top players—including Phil Taylor, Eric Bristow, John Lowe, Dennis Priestley, and Bob Anderson—formed the World Darts Council (WDC), which later became the PDC. Their aim was to modernize the sport, secure better promotion, and restore darts to its former glory.

The split from the BDO led to a bitter and lengthy legal battle over tournament names, TV rights, and player eligibility. Players who joined the new organization were banned from BDO events, and there was considerable tension between the two bodies. However, the WDC (renamed PDC in 1997) gradually built its reputation through better televised events, higher prize money, and more professional organization. By the early 2000s, the PDC had firmly established itself as the premier professional darts body.

Key pioneers like Phil Taylor, often regarded as the greatest darts player of all time, helped build the credibility of the PDC with dominant performances and global appeal. Barry Hearn, who became chairman of the PDC in 2001, played a pivotal role in transforming darts into a commercially successful sport through smart marketing and global expansion. Today, the PDC stands as a testament to the vision and risks taken by its founding players, who revolutionized the modern game.

Growth of the World Darts Championship

The World Darts Championship has seen explosive growth in popularity over the past two decades, driven by engaging broadcasts, entertaining personalities, and a festival-like atmosphere at Alexandra Palace. Broadcasters like Sky Sports and global streaming platforms have brought darts into millions of homes, helping the sport transcend its pub roots and reach international audiences. Events now attract fans from around the world, with themed costumes, lively crowds, and viral moments making darts a standout in the winter sports calendar.

As viewership has surged, so too has the prize money. In the early 2000s, the PDC World Championship offered a fraction of today's payouts. Fast forward to recent years, and the prize fund now exceeds £2.5 million, with the world champion taking home £500,000. Sponsorship deals, increased ticket sales, and international broadcasting rights have all contributed to this financial boost, making darts a lucrative career path for top players and raising the stakes of competition.

Looking ahead, the continued expansion of the sport into new markets—particularly in North America, Asia, and mainland Europe—suggests even greater potential for growth. As the PDC invests in youth development, women’s tournaments, and global tours, prize funds could increase further, possibly surpassing £3 million in the near future. With its unique blend of tradition, drama, and spectacle, the World Darts Championship seems poised to keep drawing bigger crowds, higher stakes, and global attention.

Women in Darts & Diversity in the PDC

Women’s participation in professional darts has grown significantly in recent years, with pioneers like Fallon Sherrock and Lisa Ashton leading the way. Sherrock made history at the 2019 PDC World Darts Championship by becoming the first woman to win a match, defeating both Ted Evetts and Mensur Suljović. Lisa Ashton followed with another breakthrough, becoming the first woman to earn a PDC Tour Card via Q School in 2020, allowing her to compete regularly against male professionals. These milestones helped shatter outdated perceptions and brought widespread attention to the potential of women in the sport.

To support this momentum, the PDC launched the Women’s Series in 2020, providing regular tournaments, prize money, and direct qualification routes to the World Championship for the top female players. As a result, women now have two dedicated spots in the PDC World Championship each year, and figures like Beau Greaves—WDF World Champion and rising star—are showcasing elite-level skill with averages that rival top male players.

The increased visibility of women’s darts has also been boosted by growing media coverage and sponsorship. Players like Sherrock have become highly marketable, and fans have embraced more inclusive and mixed-gender competitions. Cultural shifts within the sport have led to broader acceptance and opportunities, not only on the oche but also in roles like commentary and officiating.

While challenges remain—such as the disparity in prize money and fewer women advancing deep into major open tournaments—the direction is promising. With improved grassroots support and competitive structures, women’s darts is evolving rapidly, and it’s increasingly likely we’ll see female players making deeper runs in the biggest events of the sport.

Darts at Alexandra Palace

The Alexandra Palace, known affectionately as "Ally Pally," has been the iconic home of the PDC World Darts Championship since 2008. Every December, the venue comes alive with a raucous and colorful crowd, making it one of the most electric atmospheres in global sport. Prior to its move to Alexandra Palace, the Championship was held at the more intimate Circus Tavern in Purfleet from 1994 to 2007. While the Tavern played host to many historic moments, including several of Phil Taylor’s world titles, the sport’s rising popularity called for a grander stage.

Alexandra Palace is steeped in history and culture. Located in North London, it was originally opened in 1873 as a “People’s Palace” for public recreation and education but tragically burned down just 16 days later. It was quickly rebuilt and reopened in 1875. The palace is surrounded by nearly 200 acres of parkland and is renowned for its panoramic views over the capital. Over the decades, it has hosted everything from concerts and exhibitions to sporting events and public festivals.

One of Alexandra Palace's most significant contributions to history was serving as the site of the BBC’s first public television broadcast in 1936, making it a landmark in broadcasting. Today, it stands as both a cultural hub and a major sporting venue. Its transformation each winter into the spiritual home of darts has cemented its legacy in modern sports culture, where tradition and spectacle meet under one historic roof.
